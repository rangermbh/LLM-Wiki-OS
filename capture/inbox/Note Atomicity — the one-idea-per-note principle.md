# Note Atomicity — The One-Idea-Per-Note Principle

## Source Overview

This capture synthesizes the principle of note atomicity from multiple sources within the Personal Knowledge Management (PKM) community. The primary source is Andy Matuschak's "Evergreen Notes" framework, which articulates atomicity as one of four core properties of effective knowledge notes. Supplementary context comes from the Zettelkasten tradition (Luhmann), community discussions on the Obsidian Forum (2024), and the Linking Your Thinking (LYT) framework.

---

## Core Principle: "One Note, One Idea"

Andy Matuschak's defining rule: **each evergreen note should be about exactly one thing — but capture the entirety of that thing.** The goal is to strike a balance:

- **Too broad** → links become muddied, you miss connections when encountering new ideas about sub-notions
- **Too fragmented** → you fragment your link network, making it harder to see certain connections

As he puts it: "There's no clear litmus test or correct answer here — just a bunch of tradeoffs."

### The Software Engineering Analogy

Matuschak directly compares atomic notes to the software engineering principle of **separation of concerns** — modules should be "about one thing" to maximize reusability. Similarly, **"Evergreen note titles are like APIs"** — declarative, sharp titles that let you "invoke" a concept just by typing its name when linking. A well-crafted title becomes an abstraction handle for the entire note's idea.

---

## Why Atomicity Matters

Atomicity is one of four core properties of evergreen notes:

| Principle | Role |
|---|---|
| **Atomic** | One concept per note — enables precise linking |
| **Concept-oriented** | Organize by concept, not by book/author/project |
| **Densely linked** | Connect freely; links create emergent structure |
| **Iteratively developed** | Revisit, refine, and grow notes over time |

Atomicity **enables** dense linking — when each note captures a single concept, you can link to it from anywhere that concept appears, regardless of original context. This maximizes **reuse** and multiplies connections in your note network. Without atomicity, links become ambiguous ("which part of this mega-note am I linking to?").

---

## The Deeper Purpose

Matuschak emphasizes: **"Better note-taking misses the point; what matters is better thinking."**

Atomic note-writing is fundamentally about **developing insight over time**, not information storage. Atomic notes compound — they allow ideas to accrete across books, projects, and domains, creating surprising connections that hierarchical organization would miss. Each atomic note is a reusable "idea unit" that can participate in many different lines of thought simultaneously.

## Practical Heuristics (Community Consensus, 2024)

| Heuristic | Guidance |
|---|---|
| **Atomic ≠ short** | Atomic means "not further breakable" — not constrained to 3-5 sentences. Add detail when it supports the concept. |
| **Split when possible** | If an idea can stand alone and be linked independently, split it off. |
| **Don't split if meaning is lost** | If separation makes the reasoning hard to follow, keep ideas together. |
| **The title test** | A good atomic note's title is a complete declarative claim (e.g., "Cognitive ease influences belief," not "Notes on Thinking, Fast and Slow"). |
| **Evolve over time** | Notes aren't immutable. A concept can live inside a "parent" note until it grows enough to warrant its own note. |

## Key Tensions and Debates

### "How atomic is too atomic?"

The example of "Matrices" vs. "Addition Operator for Matrices." If a sub-note only ever appears in one parent context, merging may be more practical than forcing atomicity prematurely. The community consensus: **atomicity is a direction, not a destination.**

### "Implied links"

When context is obvious (e.g., a determinant implies matrices), should you still link both? Consensus: **only link when it adds meaningful navigational or connective value**, not out of obligation.

### Domain-Specific Considerations

In technical/scientific fields, strict atomicity can produce awkwardly granular titles like "X times A is equal to zero" — prompting some practitioners to adopt a **hybrid approach**: reference notes titled simply ("MM1 Queue") that spawn atomic insight notes with declarative claims.

## Relationship to Other Frameworks

| Framework | Relationship to Atomicity |
|---|---|
| **Zettelkasten** (Luhmann) | The historical origin. Atomicity enables the "surprising recombination" of ideas across the slip-box. Literature notes → Permanent notes pipeline. |
| **Evergreen Notes** (Matuschak) | The modern digital articulation. Adds iterative development and concept-orientation to Zettelkasten atomicity. |
| **Linking Your Thinking** (LYT) | Atomic notes + Maps of Content (MOCs) as evolving hubs. MOCs impose temporary structure on an emergent atomic note network. |
| **Note-Working** (2024) | Distinguishes atoms, molecules, and aggregates. Writing and refactoring as separate cognitive tasks. |

---

## Key Insight

**Atomicity is what makes a note network possible.** Without it, you have a document library. With it, you have a knowledge graph — where each node is precise enough to form meaningful edges, and the structure of your understanding emerges from the links between atomic ideas.
